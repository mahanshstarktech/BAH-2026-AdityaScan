Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Summary for Evaluation Committee

AdityScan is a multi-modal, physics-aware solar intelligence system that combines real observations from India's Aditya-L1 spacecraft with companion space-weather data streams to produce calibrated, uncertainty-quantified flare forecasts. The system is not a toy classifier — it is a modular, extensible sovereign space-weather forecasting architecture designed around Aditya-L1 data and extendable to Indian GEO radiation-belt monitoring via GRASP/GSAT-19.

The core pipeline is fully implemented: data ingestion from SoLEXS and HEL1OS (Aditya-L1 PRADAN archive), feature engineering with physics-motivated quantities (Neupert integral, HXR/SXR ratio, CWT QPP bands, spectral hardness index), a six-layer dilated causal Temporal Convolutional Network with cross-modal attention fusion, temperature scaling calibration, conformal prediction intervals, and ONNX export for CPU inference deployment.

Two validated performance tiers are reported: a Phase 1 lean baseline (SoLEXS + HEL1OS only, flare-heavy May 2024 window) yielding TSS 0.3752, AUC 0.6138; and a Phase 2 balanced Kaggle run (five months, quiet + active windows, Kaggle T4 GPU) yielding TSS >0.80, AUC >0.86. The improvement trajectory directly reflects the scientific insight that a balanced quiet/active training set is not optional — it is the primary driver of skill.

Each link in the chain imposes specific operational risks:

  • Hard X-ray and gamma-ray burst — energetic particle acceleration signatures.
  • Solar Energetic Particle (SEP) event — radiation dose to crewed vehicles and low-altitude assets.
  • CME shock arrival — geomagnetic storm initiation, surface-induced currents.
  • Radiation-belt electron enhancement — deep dielectric charging in GEO spacecraft, single-event effects.

1.2 Why Aditya-L1 Is Central

ISRO's Aditya-L1 mission, positioned near the Sun-Earth Lagrange point L1 (~1.5 million km), provides continuous solar observation with seven payloads spanning the electromagnetic and particle spectrum. AdityScan exploits this uniquely by treating Aditya-L1 as a multi-physics sensor stack rather than a single instrument.

Section 3

Data Sources & Sensor Modalities

3.1 SoLEXS L1 — Soft X-Ray Heartbeat

Implemented  SoLEXS (Solar Low Energy X-ray Spectrometer) provides 1-second cadence soft X-ray count-rate data from two Silicon Drift Detectors (SDD1 and SDD2) sensitive to the 1–15 keV range. In AdityScan, SoLEXS acts as the primary high-cadence flare-evolution signal — the fastest available indicator of solar plasma heating.

Physical Interpretation
A rapid positive derivative in SoLEXS count-rate indicates flare onset. Sustained elevated levels indicate hot post-flare plasma. Quiet intervals (z-score near zero, derivative near zero) are essential negative examples for model calibration.

3.2 HEL1OS L1 — Hard X-Ray Particle Acceleration

Implemented  HEL1OS (High Energy L1 Orbiting X-ray Spectrometer) measures hard X-ray emission through CdTe detectors (5–60 keV, five bands) and CZT detectors (20–160 keV, five bands). This distinguishes HEL1OS from SoLEXS in a physically critical way: soft X-rays probe thermal plasma; hard X-rays probe non-thermal electron bremsstrahlung from particle acceleration during the impulsive flare phase.

CdTe Energy Bands (Implemented Features)

  • 5–20 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 20–30 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 30–40 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 40–60 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 1.8 keV–90 keV (integral, log-scaled)

CZT Energy Bands (Implemented Features)

  • 20–40 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 40–60 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 60–80 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 80–150 keV (log-scaled count-rate)
  • 18–160 keV (integral, log-scaled)

3.3 MAG L2 — L1 Interplanetary Magnetic Field

Partially Implemented  The Aditya-L1 magnetometer provides in-situ magnetic field measurements at the L1 point. The model architecture allocates 8 MAG features: Btotal mean and standard deviation, Bx, By, Bz GSE components, clock angle, cone angle, and magnetic variance. These features are essential for the southward IMF context that drives geoeffective CME impacts.

3.4 GOES-18 XRS — External Label Stream

Implemented  NOAA GOES-18 X-Ray Sensor provides 1-minute integrated soft X-ray flux in the 1–8 Å and 0.5–4 Å channels in netCDF format. AdityScan uses GOES-18 exclusively for: (a) generating GOES flare-class labels (B/C/M/X/X+) from SWPC event lists, (b) cross-validating SoLEXS-derived X-ray states, and (c) providing a long-baseline negative-example source for balanced training.

Section 4

Physics-Informed Feature Engineering

AdityScan does not feed raw photon counts directly to a neural network. Each feature is grounded in a physical process that a domain scientist would recognise as meaningful. This is the key difference from a generic time-series classifier.

Physics-to-Feature Mapping (22-Feature Vector per Window)
Figure 4. Physics-to-feature mapping for AdityScan's 22-feature input vector. Each feature family is grounded in a documented physical process. The Neupert integral references the 1968 Neupert effect (cumulative HXR bremsstrahlung is proportional to the derivative of SXR emission). The HXR spectral index γ distinguishes thermal (γ > 4.5) from non-thermal (γ < 4.5) particle populations. CWT QPP bands use a Morlet wavelet (cmor1.5-1.0) at 8 scales spanning 1–128 seconds.

4.1 Continuous Wavelet Transform and QPP Features

Solar flares commonly exhibit quasi-periodic pulsations (QPPs) in X-ray and radio emission — oscillatory modulations with periods of seconds to minutes. These structures can encode magnetohydrodynamic oscillations and current-sheet dynamics. Raw count-rate curves in the time domain obscure these structures. AdityScan therefore applies a continuous Morlet wavelet transform (cmor1.5-1.0, 8 scales, 1–128 second period range) and extracts mean power in four QPP frequency bands as additional feature channels.

CWT Power at scale a, position b: W(a,b) = (1/√a) ∫ x(t) ψ*((t−b)/a) dt QPP feature for band k: f_qpp_k = mean( |W(a_k, b)|² ) over the 1800-s analysis window Scales used: a ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128} seconds Wavelet kernel: ψ = Complex Morlet cmor1.5-1.0 (PyWavelets)

4.2 Neupert Integral

The Neupert effect (Neupert 1968) states that the time-integrated hard X-ray emission during a flare is proportional to the soft X-ray emission rise — because accelerated electrons heat the chromospheric plasma that then evaporates to fill the SXR-emitting loop. AdityScan encodes this relationship as a real-time feature:

neupert_integral(t) = ∫₀ᵗ HXR(t') dt' Interpretation: if neupert_integral rises but SXR derivative does not, the model infers particle acceleration without proportional plasma heating → potential indicator of hard-spectrum impulsive phase.
Section 5

Machine Learning Architecture

5.1 X-Ray Causal Temporal Convolutional Network

Implemented  The X-ray branch is the fastest-response component. It consumes 1800-second (30-minute) windows of 22 X-ray and physics features and produces a 256-dimensional embedding using a six-layer dilated causal TCN.

Figure 5. X-ray Causal TCN architecture. Input: (Batch × 1800 timesteps × 22 features). Six dilated causal convolutional blocks with exponentially growing dilation (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) and kernel size 8. Channels grow 64→64→128→128→256→256. The total receptive field at the deepest layer exceeds the 30-minute window, ensuring the model has full context at every timestep. GELU activations and LayerNorm with residual connections prevent vanishing gradients.

5.2 SHARP LSTM Magnetic-History Branch

Architecture Implemented  The SHARP branch encodes 24 hours of magnetic complexity in the active region using a 2-layer LSTM. Input shape: (B, 120, 21) — 120 timesteps at 12-minute cadence, 21 SHARP + MAG-derived parameters. The final hidden state is projected through LayerNorm to a 128-dimensional embedding. This branch captures the slow free-energy build-up that can precede large flares by hours and is invisible to real-time X-ray monitoring.

5.3 In-Situ MLP Branch

Architecture Implemented  A three-layer MLP (Linear→GELU→LayerNorm×3) maps the 14-dimensional MAG + SWIS snapshot vector to a 64-dimensional embedding. This branch encodes the solar-wind and magnetic field state at L1 — information that modifies the geoeffectiveness interpretation of any flare signal. When SWIS is unavailable, those 6 slots are zero-padded and the model was trained with this masking.

5.4 Cross-Modal Attention Fusion

Figure 6. Cross-modal attention fusion schematic. The X-ray TCN embedding serves as the primary query — it is always available and provides the fastest physical signal. All branch embeddings are projected to a common 256-dimensional space. Missing modalities pass through as learned zero-context vectors rather than being dropped, allowing the model to express learned uncertainty about absent evidence. The output is a single 256-dim fused representation passed through a FFN and two residual LayerNorm layers before reaching the forecast heads.

Section 6

Calibration & Uncertainty Quantification

Raw neural network outputs are not probabilities — they are scores. For an operational space-weather system, an uncalibrated score is scientifically unacceptable. AdityScan implements a three-layer uncertainty stack:

Figure 7. AdityScan's three-layer uncertainty quantification stack. Layer 1 (temperature scaling) adjusts the probability confidence post-training. Layer 2 (conformal prediction) provides a distribution-free statistical guarantee on interval coverage. Layer 3 (MC Dropout) estimates epistemic uncertainty — whether the model is operating in a regime it has seen before. Together, these three layers produce outputs that are scientifically defensible for operational use.

Section 7

Training Strategy & Operational Infrastructure

7.1 The Golden Window Lesson

⚠️ Critical Training Insight
The May 10–20, 2024 window is physically important (it contains one of the most extreme solar events of Solar Cycle 25), but it is a poor standalone training set. With 95.99% positive window rate (only 4.01% quiet windows), the model has almost no true negative examples. It learns to predict nearly everything as flare-positive, producing a low TSS despite high POD. Balanced training is not an option — it is the primary scientific requirement.

8.1 Phase 1 — Lean Baseline (Flare-Heavy Set)

The initial training used only the May 2024 extreme event window plus limited quiet periods. The high positive window rate (95.99% in May 2024) caused the model to over-predict flares. These metrics are reported for transparency as the Phase 1 baseline:

Interpretation
The Phase 1 fusion model (TSS 0.3752, AUC 0.6138) demonstrates clear improvement over single-branch models, confirming that cross-modal attention fusion adds skill. The high FAR (0.9667) is a direct consequence of the imbalanced training set: without sufficient true negatives, the model cannot learn to suppress false alarms. This is precisely why balanced training was required.

8.2 Phase 2 — Balanced Kaggle GPU Training

After incorporating balanced quiet/active windows across four months (2024-03, 2024-05, 2024-08, 2024-09) and training on Kaggle T4 GPU with 6 epochs per month, the validation performance improved substantially. The per-month AUC results confirm that the model is learning genuine solar-state discrimination:

Section 10

GEO Radiation-Belt Extension (BAH Phase 4)

10.1 Problem Statement

The BAH 2026 energetic-particle radiation problem asks for forecasting energetic electron fluxes (>2 MeV) at geostationary orbit at lead times of 30–45 minutes, 6 hours, and 12 hours, using GOES electron flux CDF data, Wind solar-wind measurements, and ISRO GRASP/GSAT-19 data at Indian longitude.

This is not separate from AdityScan's vision — it is the downstream radiation-environment half of the same space-weather hazard chain the system is designed around.

10.2 Physics-Baseline + Residual Architecture

Figure 10. Proposed GEO radiation-belt extension architecture. The physics baseline (REFM-style linear prediction filter) provides a science-grounded prior. The residual model (Transformer or TimesFM+Cov style) learns the departures from the physics baseline. The GRASP cross-calibration module handles the energy response, spectral shape, longitude, and local-time differences between GOES electron channels and GRASP measurements — enabling an India-specific validated forecast distinct from NOAA's GOES-only operational product.

Scientific Justification for GRASP Cross-Calibration
GOES and GRASP differ in energy response, spectral coverage, detector geometry, longitude sector, and orbital environment. Treating them as identical instruments leads to systematic bias in Indian-longitude validation. The cross-calibration module fits a spectral shape from available GOES channels, converts integral flux to GRASP-comparable estimates, adds longitude/local-time correction terms, and fine-tunes on GRASP data separately. This is a defensible India-specific scientific contribution not present in any competitor approach.

TermDefinition
Aditya-L1India's first space-based solar observatory, placed near the Sun-Earth Lagrange point L1 (~1.5 × 10⁶ km from Earth), operated by ISRO

HEL1OSHigh Energy L1 Orbiting X-ray Spectrometer on Aditya-L1; CdTe (5–60 keV) and CZT (20–160 keV) hard X-ray detector
MAGAditya-L1 magnetometer; measures in-situ interplanetary magnetic field Bx/By/Bz at L1 at 10-second cadence
ASPEX-SWISSolar Wind Ion Spectrometer under the ASPEX payload on Aditya-L1; measures proton density, speed, and temperature
SUITSolar Ultraviolet Imaging Telescope on Aditya-L1; near-UV (200–400 nm) solar disk imaging
SHARPSpace-weather HMI Active Region Patch; SDO/HMI magnetogram-derived parameters describing photospheric magnetic complexity
GOES XRSX-Ray Sensor on NOAA GOES geostationary satellites; primary operational solar X-ray flux monitor; flare classification standard
GRASPGeostationary Radiation Spectrometer Payload on ISRO GSAT-19; measures charged-particle environment at Indian GEO longitude
TSSTrue Skill Statistic = POD − POFD = TPR − FPR; skill score bounded [−1, 1]; 0 = no skill; 1 = perfect; primary operational metric
HSSHeidke Skill Score; fraction of correct forecasts relative to random chance; also bounded [−∞, 1]
PODProbability of Detection = TP / (TP + FN) = sensitivity/recall
FARFalse Alarm Ratio = FP / (FP + TP); fraction of "yes" forecasts that were incorrect; lower is better
AUCArea Under the ROC Curve; 0.5 = random; 1.0 = perfect; threshold-independent measure of ranking skill
TCNTemporal Convolutional Network; uses dilated causal convolutions for time-series modeling without future data leakage
CWTContinuous Wavelet Transform; decomposes time series into time-frequency representation; used here for QPP feature extraction
QPPQuasi-Periodic Pulsation; periodic modulation in solar flare X-ray emission encoding MHD wave dynamics
IMFInterplanetary Magnetic Field; the heliospheric magnetic field measured by magnetometers at L1
GEOGeostationary Earth Orbit; ~35,786 km altitude; primary orbit for operational communication and weather satellites
ONNXOpen Neural Network Exchange; platform-neutral model format enabling CPU inference without PyTorch dependency
SEESingle-Event Effect; bit-flip or latch-up in spacecraft electronics caused by energetic particle strikes
CMECoronal Mass Ejection; large eruption of magnetised plasma from the Sun; primary driver of major geomagnetic storms
SEPSolar Energetic Particle event; high-energy protons and ions accelerated by CME shocks or flare reconnection
